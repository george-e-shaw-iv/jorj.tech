I wanted to take a second to write an article that I wish existed when I was first starting out with homebrewing with my friend.
We started homebrewing in college during COVID because we were roommates and had nothing better to do given no social events
were really happening at the time. It took us quite a while to be able to brew a recipe without a video accompanying the recipe
itself (shout out to [Joshua Weissman](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AVV3UJCFnA4)) or the recipe being explicitly written for
novices.

All of the recipes that were written for people that already understood the terminology were completely off limits to us
because we simply couldn't understand them and didn't really feel like taking the time to understand them at the time.
Eventually we did come to learn about them, and that unlocked a whole new world of what recipes we were able to follow and it
also gave us the confidence to create our own recipes or make targeted changes to existing ones. So my hope is that I can
kickstart other people's journey into homebrewing with this article.

### Terminology

This is not an exhaustive list of terminology, but it is more than enough to get you started when it comes to reading a recipe
and feeling like you can actually walk away knowing how to execute on the recipe.

* Grain Bill
    * A 'bill' (list) of mostly grains, but sometimes other things like oats, rice husks, etc. used in a beer recipe.
* Extracts
    * Extracts come in both liquid and dry form and what they allow you to do is skip the "mashing" process that you have to do
      with grain to pull fermentable sugars out of them. Extracts are basically, over-simplified, fermentable sugars from grains
      that you do not have to extract out of the grain yourself. Extracts are mostly reserved for novice brewers that want to
      skip the most finicky part of the active brewing process (mashing).
* Hops
    * Hops are plants that grow in very specific climates around the world that give beer flavor, aroma, bitterness, and help
      preserve it. Generally speaking hops are 1/3 of what make a beer a beer (grain and yeast are the other 2/3).
* Water Chemistry
    * Some recipes, not all, give water chemistry (or water profiles). What this essentially is is a set of minerals that you
      add to distilled (completely pure) water. In my opinion this is optional as a homebrewer and you're going to be just fine
      using spring water, but if you feel compelled to do this then go ahead and do it.
* Mash / Mashing
    * Mashing is the process of extracting fermentable sugars from grains. There are specific temperature ranges that activate
      enzymes in grain that helps turn the starches in the grain into fermentable sugars. The term mash refers to the active
      mixture of your grains suspended in the hot water. Generally speaking mashing lasts 60 to 90 minutes somewhere between 148
      and 158 degrees fahrenheit. Usually homebrewers suspend all of their grains (from their grain bill) in a permeable sack
      within their brew pot.
* Wort
    * The term wort refers the resulting sweet liquid after a mash (after pulling your grain sack out). Even after adding hops
      and during the fermentation process the liquid still is referred to as wort up until it is done fermenting when you can
      call it beer.
* Strike Temperature
    * Strike temperature refers to the temperature you want to get your water to before adding your grain sack to start mashing.
      Generally speaking this should always be 10 degrees fahrenheit **above** your target mash temperature, because the grains
      themselves will lower your water temperature by about that much.
* Sparging
    * Sparging is the process of extracting all of the fermentable sugars that are stuck inside of your grain sack after the
      mashing process. That grain absorbs a lot of liquid. Simply stated all this is is suspending your grain sack a little bit
      above your brew pot, squeezing the sack (you can use two dinner plates), and pouring more water through the grain sack.
      Generally you'll squeeze, pour some water, squeeze more, and do that 4-5x until not much liquid is coming out from
      squeezing.
* Boil
    * This happens after the mash (if you're using grain, if you're using all extracts you actually just skip to this step). The
      primary functions of the boil are to sterilize your brew (bacteria during fermentation is bad) and do hop additions.
      Generally speaking a boil lasts 60 minutes. Adding hops at the beginning of a boil means that you're using that hop almost
      exclusively for bittering and adding hops at the end of the boil means you're using them almost exclusively for their
      flavor and aroma properties. Adding hops somewhere in between the start and the end of the boil is essentially a gradient
      between bitterness and flavor/aroma.
* Gravity
    * Gravity is a unit of measurement that essentially means "how much fermentable sugar is in this liquid". It is usually
      taken by using an instrument called a hydrometer that is essentially a calibrated flotation device with markings on the
      side. You take a reading anytime after you've finished mashing (or adding extracts), but usually after you've cooled the
      wort down significantly. That first reading is called the original gravity. Whenever you're done fermenting you take
      another reading called the final gravity. That essentially gives you a delta of how much fermentable sugar the yeast was
      able to turn into alcohol and gives you the alcohol by volume percentage (ABV) of your beer.
* Carboy
    * A carboy is a funny word for fermenter. It is where your wort sits while the yeast is turning fermentable sugars into
      alcohol.
* Yeast
    * Yeast eats fermentable sugars and turns them into alcohol and carbon dioxide.
* Dry-hopping or Fermenting Additions
    * Dry-hopping is the act of pitching hops directly into the fermenter. Sometimes things like fruit purees or fruit peels are
      also added directly to the fermenter. I'm not sure if there is a concrete term for the latter, so we're just going to
      refer to them as fermenting additions.
* Krausen
    * Foam consisting of yeast and proteins on top of the wort in the carboy. You don't necessarily want to include this when
      bottling.
* Trub
    * Sediment that gathers at the bottom of your carboy. You don't necessarily want to include this when bottling.
* Internal Bitterness Units (IBUs)
    * A measure of how bitter a beer is. This measurement is mostly directly tied to the type and amount of bittering hops that
      you use. There are many calculators online to help you determine IBU from your recipe if you care to do so.
* Standard Reference Method (SRM)
    * A scale used to determine the final color of your beer based off of the grain bill used to brew the beer. There are plenty
      of calculators online that help you determine this if you care to do so.

#### Optional Equipment

* [Hydrometer and Cylinder](https://www.midwestsupplies.com/products/brewery-essentials-gravity-testing-kit)
    * If you don't care about knowing your ABV precisely then you don't necessarily need this, but I would grab one eventually.
      You can get somewhat close with online calculators or even plugging your recipe into an LLM, but it's never going to be
      precise because those calculators and LLMs won't know how efficient you actually were able to be in both the process of
      extracting fermentable sugars and then consuming them with the yeast.
* [Wort Chiller](https://www.midwestsupplies.com/products/silver-serpent-stainless-steel-immersion-wort-chiller)
    * Having a wort chiller that you can connect to your outdoor hose really helps a lot with bringing your wort down to the
      temperature you're going to ferment at. Without one you'll have to use an ice bath, which is a little bit annoying but
      really not that bad especially for your first few times of brewing.

### The Generic Homebrewing Process

1. Develop or find a recipe.
2. Buy the ingredients.
    * You'll need to go to your local homebrew store for grains (or extracts), hops, and yeast. If you do not have a homebrew
      store near you, you'll need to order these ingredients online at a website like
      [Midwest Supplies](https://www.midwestsupplies.com/). It is important that you need to buy your grains milled or have the
      ability to mill them yourself (most people don't - they just buy them pre-milled or have the brew store mill them).
    * Buy as much as you can at a normal grocery store to save money. Things like spring water, any fruits you may need, etc.
3. Sanitize all of your equipment using Star San sanitizer (if your equipment is especially dirty use PBW cleaner before Star
   San).
    * When I say sanitize everything, I mean everything. If you're going to use scissors to open a package of hops - those
      scissors need to be sanitized. I try to sanitize as much as I can within my brew pot while I sanitize the brew pot
      itself.
    * You'll want a dedicated brew sponge that you replace every once in a while for this process of getting the Star San or
      PBW on all of the surfaces of your brew pot, tools, and carboy.
4. Add in the pre-boil volume of your water into your brew pot.
5. Start your burner to start heating up your water with a thermometer in the pot.
6. Turn off your burner as soon as your thermometer reads your strike temperature.
    * If you end up going over you'll need to just leave the burner off with no lid on your pot and wait for it to come back
      down to strike temperature. Stirring helps bring the temperature down a little faster.
7. Lower your grain sack with milled grains into your brew pot that is currently at strike temperature. If your sack allows you,
   open it up (drape it over sides and tie it to your handles to keep it from falling in) and give it a stir every once in
   awhile. If you're not able to do that, no worries. Put the lid on and check in on the temperature every bit to make sure you
   are within a degree or two of your mash temperature - you'd be surprised at how well your temperature will hold especially in
   a 60 minute mash.
    * If you end up dropping too much in temperature from your mash temperature refire your burner, but only do it in intervals
      of 60 seconds or less. Your temperature gauge is likely going to lag behind the actual water temperature when you have
      all of your grains in there.
8. At the end of the mash you'll pull your grain sack out, suspend it above your brew pot, and start sparging (see the
   terminology section above for a quick primer on what sparging is).
    * After this you can discard the grain - you can compost it if you have the ability to do that where you live.
9. Turn your burner back on full blast and bring your wort to a boil.
10. Follow your hop schedule and add hops in at the appropriate time during the boil.
11. At the end of your boil time, turn off your burner.
12. Bring your wort down to ~73 degrees (or whatever temperature you're going to ferment at) with whatever means necessary.
    * This is the most annoying part of being a homebrewer **if you do not have a wort chiller**. What my friend and I did for
      a long time was just place the entire brew pot into a sink with a plug in it, fill the sink up with ice water, and then
      stir the brew pot. We'd replace the ice water once it fully melted/was obviously too warm to be helpful.
    * You can put it into your carboy before it gets all the way down to the temperature you're fermenting at and allow it to
      naturally cool off inside of the carboy, but ideally you don't do that very often or with too extreme ranges of
      temperature.
13. Take an original gravity reading with your hydrometer if you have one. Write this number down somewhere - you'll need it
    to calculate the ABV after fermentation concludes. You can pour the wort liquid you used to take the hydrometer reading
    back into your brew pot as long as the vessel you did it in and your hydrometer were both sanitized.
14. Transfer the wort from your brew pot into your carboy.
    * You can either use a funnel and just dump the wort through the funnel into your carboy or use your auto-siphon. Most
      people use a funnel during this step just because it is faster.
15. Pitch your yeast into the carboy.
16. Fermentation under normal conditions (at about room temperature or right above) should normally only take about 5-7 days
    depending on the type of yeast used. You'll know it's done when you stare at your airlock for a minute or two and see
    absolutely no movement (no gas escaping).
    * Don't feel pressured to get it out of the carboy as soon as fermentation stops. You can leave it in there for longer if
      you need to - honestly up to a certain point (don't go over 2 weeks) this can only help really ensure fermentation is
      completely over and really squeeze the maximum amount of ABV you can out of your fermentable sugars.
17. When you're ready to bottle, sanitize everything you need to bottle with Star San (and potentially PBW if anything is
    especially dirty for whatever reason).
    * You're going to need your brew pot, your stirring spoon, auto-siphon, bottling wand, bottles, and caps all sanitized.
    * You don't need to Star San the bottles as long you pull them directly out of the dishwasher before bottling.
18. Use a priming sugar calculator to determine how much corn sugar (or whatever you're using) you need to add to your beer
    in order to bottle carbonate your beer. You can use
    [this online calculator](https://www.brewersfriend.com/beer-priming-calculator/) (or whatever online calculator you can
    find, there are tons of them).
19. Dissolve the priming sugar in a small amount of water on the stove (get it to boiling, add the sugar, and then pull it
    from the heat).
20. Use your auto-siphon to extract all of your beer out of the carboy and into your brew pot. Be careful to not pull any of
    the trub or krausen into your brew pot, but if a little gets in it isn't that big of a deal.
21. Add your dissolved priming sugar into your brew pot and stir well.
22. Put your bottling wand onto your auto-siphon and start siphoning out of your brew pot into the bottles.
23. Cap your bottles and let rest for at least 2 weeks to allow for carbonation to take place. This should happen at the same
    temperature you fermented at.

### How to Read a Recipe

Let's try to break apart a real recipe on the internet and turn it into human words. If you want, try to do it yourself before
reading my interpretation. [Here is the recipe](https://www.brewersfriend.com/homebrew/recipe/view/23436/tank-7-clone) we're
going to use.

Let's start near the top. It says this is a 5.5 gallon batch size with a pre-boil volume of 7.5 gallons. That essentially means
this recipe wants you to start with 7.5 gallons of water and by the time you're done mashing and boiling it should be at about
5.5 gallons of wort. What that implies is that this person is using no sparging water or post-boil additive water. I almost
always modify a recipe to do both of those things if it doesn't call for them, for two reasons:

1. Sparging helps increase your efficiency as a homebrewer. Brew houses have much better equipment that allows them to extract
   a maximal amount of fermentable sugars from grains without sparging, but we do not have that luxury.
2. Adding water post-boil helps bring your temperature down much faster.

So the rule of thumb is this - you're going to lose about a gallon during mashing and another gallon during the boil. That is
already kind of implied in the recipe, but there it is plainly stated. So what I would do with this recipe is start with a
pre-boil volume of 5.5 gallons, sparge in a gallon, and then a post-boil addition of another gallon to end back up with 5.5
gallons of wort liquid before fermenting.

Moving on to the fermentables section of the recipe we see 3 things that we're going to be extracting fermentable sugar from.
Those three things are 12 lbs of american - pale ale, 3 lbs of flaked corn, and 0.30 lbs of american - white wheat. All 15.3 lbs
of those fermentables will end up going into your grain sack.

After that we see a hop schedule. The hop schedule essentially says:
* At the start of the boil add 0.20 oz of magnum hops.
* At 45 minutes left in the boil add 0.21 oz of simcoe hops.
* At 5 minutes left in the boil add 4 oz of amarillo hops.
* 14 days into fermenting (wow - this is unusual) add 0.26 oz of amarillo hops directly into the carboy.

Then we see the mash guideline. All this is saying is that you should mash for 60 minutes at 152 degrees fahrenheit. This
implies that your strike temperature should be 162 degrees fahrenheit. 

Next is the "Other Ingredients" section, which sometimes contains things to add into the carboy or some maybe things you steep
in the wort during the boil. In this recipe there is none of that, only the use of a whirlfloc tablet when there is 15 minutes
left in the boil. A whirlfloc tablet helps your beers come out more clear, so most of the time you'll want to use one unless
you're making some sort of a hazy type of beer.

The yeast section is saying to use Wyeast - Belgian Saison 3724. In the table it says that the yeast will consume approximately
78% of the fermentable sugars (so this beer shouldn't come out too dry), this is called attenuation, and optimal temperature
range to ferment at is anywhere between 70 and 95 degrees fahrenheit.

The priming section tells you that with this batch size and this yeast you're going to want to use 4oz total of corn sugar when
bottling in order to bottle carbonate your beers. Normally I just skip this section and calculate it myself when I'm bottling
using any one of the many priming sugar calculators you can find online.

Finally there is a target water profile section. I have never done water chemistry, but it gives you the breakdown of mineral
composition to add into pure distilled water if you would like to do that. I always just opt to use spring water, but maybe one
day I will care enough to do this.

### Tips for Beginners

* AI is insanely helpful for homebrewing.
    * You can use AI for a plethora of homebrewing tasks, but where it really shines is partnering with you to develop recipes,
      translating extract recipes into all-grain recipes (and vice-versa), helping you swap ingredients you can't find locally
      with ones you can find, giving you an idea of flavor profile given a recipe, helping you change existing recipes to result
      in different output sizes (e.g. if recipe was written for 10 gallons but you only have the capacity to brew 6 gallons),
      and so much more.
* Write everything down.
    * Even if you're taking a recipe verbatim from somewhere else on the internet you should write it down in your own brewing
      notebook, a google doc, or whatever works for you. If you do end up making any changes you should note those changes in
      your copy, and if you don't you at least have a place you know you can find that recipe again if you want to make it
      again (almost everyone learns this lesson the hard way - you don't have to).
* Sanitization is **extremely important**.
    * It is annoying at first, I know. But once you do it a few times you learn to do it pretty quickly yet still effectively.
      If you mess this part up and accidentally introduce bacteria into your wort you will end up with off-flavors in your beer
      which really sucks.
* Don't open your bottles prematurely.
    * Let them age for at least two weeks before cracking the first one - don't get impatient. They won't taste very good until
      two weeks passes, and they're even better after 3-4 weeks.
* Ferment in a temperature controlled place.
    * Put your carboy (and bottles as they're conditioning) in a place where you know the temperature will be controlled. What
      this usually means is just anywhere inside of your house. Don't put them somewhere like a garage where the temperature
      will vary wildly - you can produce off-flavors or even prematurely stop fermentation. 
* Brew with friends.
    * This is a genuinely fun activity to do with friends and there is a lot of downtime during the process.
* Start small and grow naturally.
    * Don't go out and buy super expensive equipment right off the bat. Start with the basics - you'll be surprised at how far
      they take you. Some people end up brewing for years and never grow out of their starter sets because there's really not a
      lot of need to do so.
* Brew stores are one of your best resources.
    * I've never met a brew store employee that wasn't eager to help someone out with a recipe or advice.
* r/Homebrewing is a great resource.
    * Made it all the way through a homebrew and then realized you forgot to buy yeast.. and it's a Sunday (so no brew stores
      are even open)? I've done that. r/Homebrewing helped by telling me that it's likely going to be okay to let the wort sit
      in the carboy and then pitch yeast a day later. It's a great resource to ask questions in a pinch - people are very nice
      usually.
