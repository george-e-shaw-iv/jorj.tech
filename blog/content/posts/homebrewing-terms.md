### Terminology

* Grain Bill
    * A 'bill' (list) of mostly grains, but sometimes other things like oats, rice husks, etc. used in a beer recipe.
* Extracts
    * Extracts come in both liquid and dry form and what they allow you to do is skip the "mashing" process that you have to do
      with grain to pull fermentable sugars out of them. Extracts are basically, over-simplified, fermentable sugars from grains
      that you do not have to extract out of the grain yourself. Extracts are mostly reserved for novice brewers that want to
      skip the most finnicky part of the active brewing process (mashing).
* Hops
    * Hops are plants that grow in very specific climates around the world that give beer flavor, aroma, bitterness, and help
      preserve it. Generally speaking hops are 1/2 of what make a beer a beer (grain is the other 1/2).
* Water Chemistry
    * Some recipes, not all, give water chemistry (or water profiles). What this essentially is is a set of minerals that you
      add to distilled (completely pure) water. In my opinion this is optional as a homebrewer and you're going to be just fine
      using spring water, but if you feel compelled to do this then go ahead and do it.
* Mash / Mashing
    * Mashing is the process of extracting fermentable sugars from grains. There are specific temperature ranges that activate
      enzymes in grain that helps turn the starches in the grain into fermentable sugars. The term mash refers to the active
      mixture of your grains suspended in the hot water. Generally speaking mashing lasts 60 to 90 minutes somewhere between 148
      and 158 degrees fahrenheit. Usually homebrewers suspend all of their grains (from their grain bill) in a permeable sack
      within their brew pot.
* Wort
    * The term wort refers the resulting sweet liquid after a mash (after pulling your grain sack out). Even after adding hops
      and during the fermentation process the liquid still is referred to as wort up until it is done fermenting when you can
      call it beer.
* Strike Temperature
    * Strike temperature refers to the temperature you want to get your water to before adding your grain sack to start mashing.
      Generally speaking this should always be 10 degrees fahrenheit **above** your target mash temperature, because the grains
      themselves will lower your water temperature by about that much.
* Boil
    * The happens after the mash (if you're using grain, if you're using all extracts you actually just skip to this step). The
      primary functions of the boil are to sterilize your brew (bacteria during fermentation is bad) and do hop additions.
      Generally speaking a boil lasts 60 minutes. Adding hops at the beginning of a boil means that you're using that hop almost
      exclusively for bittering and adding hops at the end of the boil means you're using them almost exclusively for their
      flavor and aroma properties. Adding hops somewhere in between the start and the end of the boil is essentially a gradient
      between bitterness and flavor/aroma.
* Gravity
    * Gravity is a unit of measurement that essentially means "how much fermentable sugar is in this liquid". It is usually
      taken by using an instrument called a hydrometer that is essentially a calibrated flotation device with markings on the
      side. You take a reading anytime after you've finished mashing (or adding extracts), but usually after you've cooled the
      wort down significantly. That first reading is called the original gravity. Whenever you're done fermenting you take
      another reading called the final gravity. That essentially gives you a delta of how much fermentable sugar the yeast was
      able to turn into alcohol and gives you the alcohol by volume percentage (ABV) of your beer.
* Carboy
    * A carboy is a funny word for fermenter. It is where your wort sits while the yeast is turning fermentable sugars into
      alcohol.
* Adjuncts
    * Anything you end up putting into the carboy directly (not adding it to the wort during mashing or boiling) is called
      an adjunct. Most commonly this is fruit or more hops.

### The Homebrewing Process, Simply Stated

1. Develop or find a recipe.
2. Buy the ingredients.
    * You'll need to go to your local homebrew store for grains (or extracts), hops, and yeast. If you do not have a homebrew
      store near you, you'll need to order these ingredients online at a website like
      [Northern Brewer](https://www.northernbrewer.com/). It is important that you need to buy your grains milled or have the
      ability to mill them yourself (most people don't - they just buy them pre-milled or have the brew store mill them).
    * Buy as much as you can at a normal grocery store to save money. Things like spring water, any fruits you may need, etc.
3. Sanitize all of your equipment using Star San sanitizer (if your equipment is especially dirty use PBW cleaner before Star
   San).
    * When I say sanitize everything, I mean everything. If you're going to use scissors to open a package of hops - those
      scissors need to be sanitized. I try to sanitize as much as a I can within my brew pot while I sanitize the brew pot
      itself.
    * You'll want a dedicated brew sponge that you replace every once in awhile for this process of getting the Star San or
      PBW on all of the surfaces of your brew pot, tools, and carboy.
4. Add in the pre-boil volume of your water into your brew pot.
5. Start your burner to start heating up your water with a thermometer in the pot.
6. Turn off your burner as soon as your thermometer reads your strike temperature.
    * If you end up going over you'll need to just leave the burner off with no lid on your pot and wait for it to come back
      down to strike temperature. Stirring helps bring the temperature down a little faster.

### Tips for Beginners

* AI is insanely helpful for homebrewing.
    * You can use AI for a plethora of homebrewing tasks, but where it really shines is partnering with you to develop recipes,
      translating extract recipes into all-grain recipes (and vice-versa), helping you swap ingredients you can't find locally
      with ones you can find, giving you an idea of flavor profile given a recipe, helping you change existing recipes to result
      in different output sizes (e.g. if recipe was written for 10 gallons but you only have the capacity to brew 6 gallons),
      and so much more.
* Write everything down.
    * Even if you're taking a recipe verbatim from somewhere else on the internet you should write it down in your own brewing
      notebook, a google doc, or whatever works for you. If you do end up making any changes you should note those changes in
      your copy, and if you don't you at least have a place you know you can find that recipe again if you want to make it
      again (almost everyone learns this lesson the hard way - you don't have to).
* Brew with friends.
    * This is a genuinely fun activity to do with friends and there is a lot of downtime during the process.
* Start small and grow naturally.
    * Don't go out and buy super expensive equipment right off the bat. Start with the basics - you'll be surprised at how far
      they take you. Some people end up brewing for years and never grow out of their starter sets because there's really not a
      lot of need to do so.
* Brew stores are one of your best resources.
    * I've never met a brew store employee that wasn't eager to help someone out with a recipe or advice.
* r/Homebrewing is a great resource.
    * Made it all the way through a homebrew and then realized you forgot to buy yeast.. and it's a Sunday (so no brew stores
      are even open)? I've done that. r/Homebrewing helped by telling me that it's likely going to be okay to let the wort sit
      in the carboy and then pitch yeast a day later. It's a great resource to ask questions in a pinch - people are very nice
      usually.
